1. Market Analysis

1.1 The .NET Enterprise Market

The .NET ecosystem represents one of the largest enterprise development platforms globally, with Microsoft reporting over 5 million active .NET developers. Key market characteristics:

  • Enterprise Dominance: .NET is the primary platform for Fortune 500 companies, particularly in finance, healthcare, and government sectors.

  • Self-Hosted Preference: Post-pandemic, 67% of enterprises prefer on-premises or private cloud solutions for sensitive data (Gartner 2024).

2.4 Strategic Implication: Support ASP.NET Core MVC Too

Critical Recommendation: Do NOT position as "Blazor-only". Your product should work with:
  1. Blazor Server - Primary target, interactive apps
  2. Blazor WebAssembly - Client-side scenarios
  3. ASP.NET Core MVC/Razor Pages - Huge installed base, often overlooked
  4. Blazor Hybrid (MAUI) - Desktop/mobile apps

By supporting MVC/Razor Pages, you access the ENTIRE .NET web developer market (~2-3 million developers), not just Blazor (~300-500K). This 5-10x increase in addressable market is crucial for viability.

3.4 Why Proprietary (Not Open Source) Is Right for You

Open Source Risk for Solo Founders:

Open source works for companies with VC funding to outlast forks, network effects (GitLab, Elastic), or complex enterprise features forks can't replicate.

For a solo founder, open source is risky:

  • A well-funded competitor could fork your work, add polish, and out-market you
  • Partners and consultancies might just use the free version and never pay
  • You lose the source code licensing revenue stream (high margin)
  • Support burden increases dramatically with free users who expect help

Recommendation: Stay proprietary. Offer source code as a premium paid option, not free. Your concerns about forking are valid and legitimate.

12.3 My Recommendations

Do This:

  1. Keep your day job initially. Build and launch as a side project until you have $50K ARR and 6 months runway.
  2. Launch Forms first, perfect it. Don't split focus. Workflow comes after Forms has traction.
  3. Target ISVs and consultancies first. Shorter sales cycles, technical buyers, good references for enterprise.
  4. Invest in documentation. Excellent docs reduce support burden and build trust.
  5. Build in public. Share your journey on Twitter/X, LinkedIn - creates connection and early adopters.
  6. Stay proprietary. Keep full control of your codebase. Offer source code as premium paid option, not free.

  8. Get early customers at a discount. Trade lower price for testimonials, case studies, feedback.

Avoid This:

  1. Don't undercharge. $99/year won't attract enterprise - it signals "hobby project."
  2. Don't over-promise timelines. Enterprise buyers understand software takes time. Be honest.
  3. Don't chase government contracts first. They're appealing but will bankrupt you waiting.
  4. Don't ignore support infrastructure. One angry enterprise customer can damage you.
  5. Don't build everything yourself. Use AI (like me!) extensively. Automate everything possible.
  6. Don't open source the core. Risk of forks, freeloading partners, and lost source code revenue.

14. Critical Mistakes That Will Kill This Business

These are not hypotheticals. Each mistake below has killed similar businesses. Avoid them at all costs.

14.1 Product Mistakes (Business Killers)

Mistake Why It Kills Prevention
Shipping a buggy V1 First impressions permanent. Enterprise devs won't give second chances. Bad reviews last forever. Delay launch until core functionality is solid. Better late and good than early and broken.
Feature bloat before core is solid Adding features while bugs exist creates compounding instability. Users don't need 50 features, they need 10 that work perfectly. Freeze features after MVP. Fix every bug before adding anything new. "Boring but solid" beats "exciting but broken".
Ignoring performance Forms with 50+ fields will lag. Workflow designer will freeze. Users will abandon and never return. Performance test with 100+ fields from day one. Virtualize lists. Profile regularly.
Breaking changes without migration path Enterprise customers have years of schemas. Breaking them = angry customers = refunds = reputation damage. Semantic versioning. Migration tools. Never break without 6+ month deprecation notice.
Poor upgrade experience NuGet update that breaks things = support nightmare. Customers stuck on old versions = fragmentation hell. Automated upgrade testing. Changelog for every release. Zero-breaking-change minor versions.

14.2 Business Model Mistakes (Slow Death)

Mistake Why It Kills Prevention
Underpricing $99/year signals "hobby project". Enterprise won't trust it. You can't afford support. Race to bottom. Minimum $199 for any tier. Enterprise minimum $2,500. Price signals value.
Free tier too generous If free does everything, why pay? Attracts freeloaders who demand support but never convert. Free tier: 3 fields or read-only designer. Enough to evaluate, not enough to use in production.
Chasing government first 12-36 month sales cycles. While waiting, you run out of money. Even if you win, payment is 90+ days. Target ISVs/consultancies first (1-3 month cycles). Government is Year 2-3 play.
No support boundaries Enterprise customers expect unlimited support. Without boundaries, one customer consumes all your time. Clear SLA per tier. Community/email/priority tiers. Implementation services are paid add-ons.
Custom development trap One customer offers $20K for custom features. You spend 2 months. Product stalls. Other customers leave. Custom work only if: (a) feature benefits all customers, (b) 50% paid upfront, (c) won't delay roadmap.

14.3 Marketing Mistakes (Invisibility)

Mistake Why It Kills Prevention
Build and they will come They won't. Discovery is the hardest problem. 99% of developer tools die in obscurity. Marketing starts before launch. Build email list. Create content. Build in public.

14.5 The Cascade of Failure

How Businesses Actually Die:
  1. Launch with bugs (rushed, no testing)
  2. Bad reviews appear, word spreads in tight .NET community
  3. Sales slow, panic sets in
  4. Drop prices to attract customers (desperation pricing)
  5. Attract price-sensitive customers who demand support
  6. Support burden explodes, no time for product improvement
  7. Product falls behind, remaining good customers leave
  8. Revenue drops, can't afford to continue
  9. Abandon project, joining the 90% of dev tools that fail

Prevention: Break the chain early. Don't rush launch. Don't underprice. Don't over-promise support. Maintain sustainable pace.

14.6 The One Mistake That Trumps All Others

The Fatal Mistake: Giving Up Too Early

Most developer tools that eventually succeed looked like failures at month 12. The difference between success and failure is often simply persistence through the trough of sorrow.

The Timeline Reality:

  • Months 1-6: Excitement, launch, initial users
  • Months 7-18: The Trough - growth stalls, motivation drops, everything feels hard
  • Months 19-36: Gradual acceleration (if you survive the trough)
  • Year 3+: Compounding growth kicks in

The Commitment Test: Before starting, ask yourself: "Am I willing to work on this for 3 years even if Year 1 revenue is only $20K?" If the answer is no, reconsider. If yes, you have the mindset to succeed.
