Business Plan: .NET Enterprise Forms & Workflow Platform

Executive Summary

This business plan outlines the commercial strategy for a proprietary Blazor Dynamic Forms System integrated with a robust Metadata-Driven Workflow Engine. The target market is the "Microsoft Enterprise" niche (Government, Banking, Healthcare, and Large Corps) that requires Data Sovereignty (Self-Hosted SQL Server) and Source Code Ownership.

Unique Value Proposition: Unlike SaaS competitors (PowerApps, JotForm), this solution offers an on-premise, developer-friendly architecture with advanced delegation capabilities ("Workbenches") specifically designed for rigid organizational hierarchies.

3. Longevity & Exit Strategy Analysis

Can this survive for 10 years?

Yes, with caveats.

  • The "Sticky" Factor: Once an Enterprise implements your WorkflowAuth1 schema and fills it with 5 years of legal approval history, they will never migrate away unless the technology becomes completely obsolete.
  • Technology Risk: Blazor is Microsoft's long-term bet. As long as .NET exists (which it will for decades), this system remains viable. The SQL backend is timeless.
  • Maintenance Mode: Even if you stop adding features in Year 5, the "Software Assurance" revenue (Tier 3 renewals) can provide passive income for years just to keep dependencies updated.

Exit Strategy: Who buys this?

This is a classic "Acqui-hire" or "Technology Acquisition" target.

  1. Component Vendors (Telerik, Syncfusion, DevExpress): They sell UI controls but lack robust "Business Logic" engines. Buying your Workflow Engine to bundle with their suites is a logical move to increase their Average Revenue Per User (ARPU).
  2. Consultancies (Accenture, Deloitte, local .NET shops): These firms build this exact system from scratch for every client. Buying your IP to use as their internal "Accelerator" allows them to bid lower and faster on government contracts.
  3. Private Equity (Micro-SaaS): If you reach ~$300k-$500k ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue), micro-PE firms will be interested in buying the cash flow for a 3x-4x multiple ($1.5M - $2M exit).

4. Conclusion

The combination of Dynamic Forms (UI) and Workflow Engine (Logic) creates a complete "Digital Transformation in a Box" for the .NET ecosystem. By targeting the high-compliance Enterprise market with a "Source Code" model, you avoid competing directly with low-cost SaaS tools and position the product as a strategic infrastructure asset. The project is financially viable as a high-margin boutique software business with a strong path to a lucrative exit within 5-7 years.

6. Marketing & Sales Strategy (Solo Founder Edition)

Constraint: No sales team, limited budget. Goal: High-trust, low-touch sales for Tiers 1 & 2, and "Founder-Led Sales" for Tier 3.

A. The "Trojan Horse" Strategy (Targeting Developers)

Enterprises don't buy software; their Senior Architects recommend it. You must win the technical influencers first.

  • The "Open Core" Lite Version: Release a free, stripped-down version of the Form Runner (Nuget package) that renders JSON to HTML. It solves a small problem for free. The "Upgrade" is the Visual Designer and Workflow Engine.
  • Content Marketing (Technical Deep Dives): Write articles like "How to implement Role-Based Workbenches in Blazor" or "Handling 500-field Forms in SQL Server." Post these on LinkedIn, .NET Subreddits, and Blazor forums. Prove you are the expert.
  • Blazor Component Vendors Marketplace: List your product as a "Partner Add-on" for Telerik/Syncfusion if possible, or optimize SEO for "Blazor Form Builder" to catch developers looking for what the big vendors are missing.

B. Government & Enterprise Sales (Canada & USA)

Selling to Gov/Corp without a sales team requires navigating procurement processes efficiently.

1. The "Vendor of Record" Hack (Canada Focus)

Government procurement is rigid. You cannot just "call them."

  • Partner with System Integrators (SIs): Do not sell to the government directly. Sell to the consultancies (e.g., CGI, Deloitte, local Microsoft Gold Partners) who already have the government contracts.
  • The Pitch to SIs: "You are bidding on a $2M portal project. Don't build the form engine from scratch. Use my engine for $25k, and your margin goes up by $100k."
  • Benefits: The SI handles the sales, legal, and support relationship with the government. You just provide the software license.

2. The "Data Sovereignty" Wedge (US & Cross-Border)

Use American compliance laws to your advantage.

  • Marketing Message: "The only Form Builder that guarantees your data never leaves your own SQL Server." (Targeting HIPAA/ITAR compliance).
  • Marketplaces: List on the Azure Marketplace. Even though it's on-prem software, CIOs often look there for approved vendors, and it simplifies billing (they pay Microsoft, Microsoft pays you).

3. The "Price Friction" Paradox (Why Higher is Easier)

Strategy: Avoiding "No Man's Land" pricing.

  • The Reality: It is often harder to sell a $2,000 tool (which requires manager justification but looks like a "toy") than a $25,000 platform (which fits into "Capital Expenditure" budgets and signals stability).
  • The "Sole Source" Advantage: By defining your product specifications narrowly and uniquely ("The only Source-Available .NET Workflow Engine with Workbench Delegation"), you can often qualify as a Sole Source Provider. This allows government agencies to bypass the mandatory "3 Competitive Bids" process, speeding up the sales cycle significantly.
  • Action: Do not discount Tier 3. The high price validates the software's role as critical infrastructure.

5. Managing SI Partner Risk

Risk: Partners stealing the IP to build an internal competitor.

  • The "Technical Debt" Defense: Position the product maintenance (upgrades, security patches, browser compatibility) as a cost they don't want. "Why spend $300k/year on internal R&D when you can pay me $25k?"
  • License, Not Transfer: Contracts explicitly grant usage rights for clients but forbid repackaging as a standalone competing product.
  • Rapid Iteration: By the time they reverse-engineer V1, you are releasing V3. Be the specialist they rely on, not just a vendor.

C. The "Legacy Bridge" Strategy (MVC/Core Expansion)

Strategic Goal: Unlock the massive market of legacy ASP.NET MVC apps without competing directly with commodity JS libraries (like SurveyJS).

  • The Product Expansion: Develop a vanilla JavaScript/Web Component renderer that consumes your JSON schema. This allows legacy MVC apps to render forms without a full Blazor rewrite.
  • The Positioning Defense: Do not market this as a "JS Form Library." Market it as a ".NET-Native Platform with a JS Client."
    • SurveyJS/Others: Require separate Node.js backends or SaaS APIs. Friction for .NET shops.
    • Your Solution: Runs natively inside the existing ASP.NET App Pool. Uses existing SQL Transactions and Identity. Zero infrastructure changes.
  • The Value Prop: "Modernize your user-facing forms today using our JS renderer, while keeping your rock-solid .NET backend logic untouched."

D. The "High-Touch" Illusion (Simulating a Team)

  • Automated Demos: Record a high-quality, 10-minute "Deep Dive" video. Gate it behind an email signup. This acts as your "Sales Rep 1".
  • Documentation as Sales Tool: Enterprise buyers judge risk by documentation quality. Have a public "Developer Docs" site that looks incredibly professional. It proves the system is maintainable.
  • Pricing Transparency (Tier 1 & 2): Put the price on the website. Solo founders waste time negotiating $3k deals. Let them buy with a credit card. Only "Call for Pricing" on the Tier 3 ($25k+) deals.

E. Fractional Sales & Channel Partners (No-Salary Sales Team)

Strategy: Leveraging "Rolodex Reps" and MSPs instead of hiring full-time sales staff.

  • The Model: Engage semi-retired enterprise sales veterans or specialized MSPs (Managed Service Providers) who already have relationships with your target CIOs.
  • The Offer: Aggressive Commission (20-30%) in exchange for Zero Base Salary.
    • Example: On a $25,000 Tier 3 deal, the rep earns $7,500. You keep $17,500. This is high-margin "found money" with zero risk.
  • Role Split: The Rep opens the door and handles the contract negotiation (The "Closer"). You perform the technical demo (The "Sales Engineer").

7. Roadmap: Beyond the Core (Years 3-7)

A common concern is "running out of features" after the core Form Runner and Workflow Engine are built. However, Enterprise revenue is driven by Integration, Compliance, and Content, not just new engine mechanics. The development focus shifts as follows:

Phase A: The "Enterprise Wrappers" (Years 3-4)

Deepening the product to meet strict Corporate/Government requirements.

  • Advanced Audit Logs: "Who changed field X on date Y?" Building a forensic history viewer.
  • Identity Provisioning (SCIM): Auto-syncing users from Azure AD/Okta when hired/fired.
  • Accessibility (WCAG 2.1): Perfecting screen reader support and keyboard navigation (Mandatory for Gov).
  • Localization: RTL support (Arabic/Hebrew) and multi-language designer interfaces.

Phase B: The "Connectors" Ecosystem (Years 4-5)

Selling "Integration Packs" to connect forms to the rest of the business.

  • SharePoint/Office 365: Save form data directly to SharePoint Lists or OneDrive.
  • Teams/Slack: Approval notifications and "ChatOps" workflow actions.
  • Salesforce/Dynamics 365: Two-way data binding (pull customer data into forms).
  • Payment Gateways: Stripe/PayPal integration for "Paid Forms" (Permits, Licenses).

Phase C: Vertical Solutions & Content (Years 5+)

Stopping the sale of "Tools" and starting the sale of "Solutions".

  • "The HR Pack": Pre-wired Onboarding, Leave Requests, and Performance Reviews.
  • "The IT Service Pack": Asset Requests, Access Control, Incident Reporting.
  • "The Gov/Muni Pack": FOIA Requests, Permit Applications, Citizen Reporting.
  • AI Integration: Auto-summarizing complex forms for approvers; AI-assisted form generation.
